---
title: Your single-page website is a pamphlet
date: 2026-05-12
description: One page is a statement. Multiple pages are a commitment. Make the commitment.
---

There's a genre of website that's become very fashionable among developers. It has a beautiful hero section. A clean sans-serif font. Maybe a dark mode. It loads fast. It looks great on a retina display. And it has exactly one page.

I have a word for these websites: pamphlets.

A pamphlet is something you hand to a stranger at an airport and then disappear. It answers one question — usually "what is this thing?" — and then it's done with you. You can't return to it. You can't follow its thinking over time. You can't find the piece it wrote three months ago that's relevant to the thing you're researching today.

A website is different. A website is somewhere a person *lives* on the internet. It accumulates. It grows. It has history. When you visit someone's website two years after you first found them, you can see what they've been thinking about. That's worth something.

---

Here's the practical problem with one-page websites: they disappear from your memory.

You find something interesting. You want to come back to it later. You try to remember where you saw it. Was it Twitter? Was it a newsletter? Was it that developer's website? You can't remember, because the website has no shape — it's just a page, not a place.

A multi-page site has landmarks. You remember "that person who wrote about X." You go to their website. You look at their blog. You find the post.

One page gives you nowhere to come back to.

---

I'm not saying you need a 47-page documentation site with a versioned changelog and four translation teams. I'm saying: write some things. Put them somewhere. Give people a reason to visit more than once.

A blog doesn't have to be updated weekly. It can have three posts. Three posts is three more pieces of evidence that you're a person who thinks about things and writes them down.

An about page doesn't have to be a full biography. It can be five sentences. Five sentences is the difference between "who is this person?" and "oh, okay."

A /now page takes 15 minutes to write and tells people more about you than any bio section you've ever agonized over.

---

The bar is very low. The bar is: have more than one page.

That's it. Clear the bar.
